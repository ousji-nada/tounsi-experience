The Experience

Cook. Laugh.
Eat Together.

Lella Fatma has been making couscous every Friday for 60 years. She learned from her mother, who learned from hers. Today, she's learning you — and you're in very good hands.

You'll gather around a low table in her sunny home in Ariana, a quiet suburb of Tunis that tourists almost never reach. First comes the semolina — coarse, sun-dried Tunisian durum wheat — spread in a wide earthenware dish called a gsaa. Then, with slightly wet hands, you'll begin to roll. It takes patience. Lella Fatma will correct your technique at least three times, with the calm authority of someone who has been perfecting this for six decades.

While the couscous steams twice in the couscoussier, the broth builds — lamb shoulder, seven vegetables, ras el hanout, dried rose petals. The kitchen fills with a warmth that has nothing to do with the stove. You'll share lunch together, the meal you made with your own hands, and leave with a full stomach, a recipe booklet, and the name of someone in Tunisia you'll call a friend.

What's Included
